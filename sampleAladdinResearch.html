Summary of Trip to Argentina - July 2 & 3 (Buenos Aires, BTG)
Will Landers and Andrea Cardia

We spent two days visiting with YPF, Banco Macro, Galicia, Clarin, representatives from the three main presidential candidates' camps and reporters. Andres Borentstein, chief Argentina economist for BTG Pactual, organized our schedule. The conclusions from the trip were the following: a. a resolution with the holdouts should happen sooner rather than later - Argentina needs to regain access to international capital markets and $15b (3% of GDP) should not stand in the way of a resolution; b. President Cristina Kirchner is unlikely to move away from recent orthodox policies - she is working to ensure her political relevance post October 2015 elections; and c. all three main presidential candidates should follow market friendly policies, but changes will be gradual and single digit inflation will be a 2017/18 reality at the earliest.

Assuming the successful negotiation with debt holdouts, there is an expectation tha companies and provinces returning to international debt markets as quickly as feasible. In addition, there are supposedly some 10-12 Argentine companies that would be ready to tap the equity market should conditions allow (post elections?). A complete removal of capital controls seems a bit away, so hard to envision local equity offerings attracting foreign investors.

We had a lunch with 4 members of Daniel Scioli's team on July 3rd. They are currently employed by Banco de la Provincia de Buenos Aires (headed by the president of the bank, Santiago Montoya, who would be a senior cabinet member if they win the election). Mr. Scioli is the current governor of Province of Buenos Aires, a Peronist but not a Kirchnista. This meeting was the least clear in terms of policies to be followed - with a lot of focus given to the need to staying true to Peronist values (working with the unions, control of power, etc). The group was very vocal about following a strategy of continuity with change (but not very clear about how that would work). They stressed the need to have a positive impact on the economy ahead of midterm elections in 2017 (18 months after taking office), but stressed that the adjustments will be gradual and take a long time to take full impact and that they would be unwilling to throw the economy into a deeper recession in order to get things on track more quickly due to the political cost. He is currently seen as the front runner, but the policies to be followed were not clear from this meeting.

Later we met with two partners in the consulting firm Analytica - Ricardo Delgado and Rodrigo. They are advisors to the Sergio Massa campaign. Mr. Massa is currently a Congressman, also a Peronist and former chief of staff under President Nestor Kirchner, and a vocal critic of the current government. They had the most clear path after taking office, understandings how critical the first 100 days in office are. The three priorities would be to tackle inflation and the government deficit (focusing primarily on the 5% of GDP currently spent in subsidies, mostly in energy), strengthening the oil law to attract more investment (somewhat critical of YPF -and its president Gallucio ""he acts as the Energy Minister, not the president of a company"") and encouraging infrastructure investments.

On July 2nd, we had lunch with four advisors to Mauricio Macri, the current mayor of Buenos Aires and main non-Peronist candidate. They emphasized the need for infrastructure investments - focusing heavily on PPPs. The most disappointing part of the meeting was that, when asked, they could not illustrate how their candidate differs from the two Peronist candidates, stating that the campaign would not be about that. Also stated that they are still working on an economic plan that would allow Argentina to move away from the historical boom and bust cycle, with whatever gains from the first two years in office lasting longer (but again unclear how this plan would work). Indicated the willingness (likelihood?) of joining forces with the Radicalist party closer to the election - but not at this stage (would join forces at Provincial elections in exchange for running together at the federal level - radicals seem to also have similar market friendly plans for the economy.

We had breakfast with two journalists that attempted to clarify reasons behind Argentine voters' unwavering support for Peronist candidates. After a while, the conclusion was that Argentines have a deep patriotic pride that they feel is challenged by foreigners (especially the Brits going back to railroad investments that were seen as a way to more quickly removing natural resources away from Argentina, and over the past 30 years focused on the Falkland Islands; but also Americans) desire to knock down Argentina ?!?!? As a result, a focus on populist and nationalistic rhetorics works with a significant portion of the electorate. The only reason for President Kirchner's move to more orthodoxy in the past year was financial - Argentina was burning through reserves, the trade balance was dwindling, and the country needs to regain access to international capital markets. Energy shortages and energy subsidies are a major drag on the economy, and the country needs private capital to invest in the energy and infrastructure sector. President Kirchner wants to finish her government on a positive note to ensure her political relevance in order to minimize potential legal problems after leaving office.

YPF meeting - in line with recent meetings. Company is strong liquidity shape in case of a technical default. Around 30 wells in Vaca Muerta have hit the ""sweet spot"" and are very productive. No hurry to sign any new agreements there, but negotiations are on going with Petronas (and other players may step in if a deal is close to being signed). Too early to speak about a potential sovereign fund given potential success of Vaca Muerta. Company has instituted a stock bonus plan based on yearly metrics that cover approximately 500 employees.

Banco Macro - strong focus on government employees and payroll lending. Growth more or less in line with inflation - no mortgage market to speak of. Concerned that the government may institue mandate lending to consumer market as it has done with corporate market. Under a more normalized macro scenario, Macro would most likely speed up growth via acquisition in the region of Buenos Aires and grow fast organically outside BA, in provinces where it currently has large presence. The bank has historically grown through acquisition and is well capitalized to take advantage of a possible consolidation process the banking sector in Argentina should go through under stable macro conditions.

Galicia - more focused on consumer lending via credit cards and low income segments via its two subsidiaries - Tarjetas Regionales and CFA. Not active in payroll lending. Gaining market share in deposit market, which funds growth in lending. CFA impacted by interest rate caps implemented three weeks ago - moved model from direct lending to credit cards where the caps are higher. Tarjetas Regionales could eventually be IPOed in a normalized macro environment, funding growth for both the banking business and credit card business.

Clarin - big target of the government - being forced to break up the group. Not allowed to hold cable and open broadcast channels in the same market. Will break the listed company into two listed entities, and sell four other units. Don't seem to have a lot of friends with opposition candidates, either. Not investable!
